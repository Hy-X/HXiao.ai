When Geoffrey Hinton introduced knowledge distillation in 2015, the core insight was deceptively simple: a trained neural network knows more than its predictions reveal. The probability distribution over all classes — not just the winning prediction — encodes structural relationships that can be transferred to a smaller model.

These "soft labels" carry what Hinton called "dark knowledge." A model that assigns 0.7 probability to class A, 0.2 to class B, and 0.1 to class C is communicating something richer than a hard one-hot label ever could: it is expressing the similarity structure of the input space.

The Mechanics of Distillation

Training a student model under distillation combines two objectives. The first is standard cross-entropy against ground truth labels. The second — weighted by a temperature hyperparameter — is the KL divergence between the student's soft outputs and the teacher's soft outputs at elevated temperature.

Temperature scaling softens both distributions, amplifying the signal in the low-probability tails and giving the student richer gradient information about the structure of the problem.

In practice, the temperature parameter T is typically swept between 2 and 10. Higher values transfer more relational structure; lower values keep the student closer to hard decisions. The right balance is task-specific.

Applications in Scientific Machine Learning

In seismological applications — where I work on P-wave and S-wave detection models — distillation offers a practical path to field deployment. A large teacher network, trained on full Oklahoma datasets with high-capacity architectures, can supervise a compact student that runs within a microcontroller's 320KB RAM constraint.

The student inherits the teacher's calibration and its understanding of borderline cases — exactly the events that matter most for real-time monitoring. In field tests with distilled PNet variants, we observed accuracy preservation above 95% of the teacher's performance at a fraction of the compute and memory footprint.

Distillation vs. Quantization

Distillation and quantization are complementary, not competing. Distillation compresses the model's capacity; quantization compresses its numerical representation. Used together — distill first, then quantize — they produce models that are both architecturally lean and numerically compact, maximally suited for edge deployment scenarios where both RAM and flash are limited.
