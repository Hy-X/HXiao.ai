Every seismologist who has attempted to apply a model trained on California tectonic earthquakes to Oklahoma induced seismicity has encountered the same uncomfortable result: performance degrades, sometimes dramatically. The waveform statistics are different. The magnitude-frequency distributions are different. The focal mechanisms are different.

This is domain shift — the gap between the distribution a model was trained on and the distribution it is asked to generalize to. Transfer learning does not eliminate domain shift, but it provides principled tools for adapting across it.

What Actually Transfers

In deep neural networks for seismic phase picking, the lower layers (closer to input) learn generic waveform features: onset sharpness, frequency content, amplitude envelope shapes. These tend to transfer well across seismic domains because P and S waves share physical characteristics regardless of tectonic setting.

The higher layers encode more domain-specific decision boundaries. These often need retraining — or at minimum, fine-tuning — when applying to a new seismic region.

A practical approach is to freeze the first two-thirds of the encoder and retrain only the decoder and final classification layers on the target domain dataset. This preserves the general waveform representations while allowing the model to adjust to the statistical differences in the target distribution.

Oklahoma as a Target Domain

Oklahoma's induced seismicity — driven primarily by wastewater injection from oil and gas operations — has unique signatures. Events tend to cluster in space and time, magnitude ranges skew lower, and the regional velocity model differs substantially from California. Models pretrained on the STEAD or SCSN catalogs require careful adaptation before they perform at analyst level on OGS network data.

In my work with distilled PNet models, I found that training on a regionally curated Oklahoma subset — even a relatively small one of a few thousand labeled events — closed roughly 80% of the performance gap relative to a full-retraining baseline. The remaining gap was largely in very low-magnitude detection where SNR is inherently challenging.

Data Efficiency and Few-Shot Adaptation

One of transfer learning's greatest practical advantages is data efficiency. Fully supervised training of a phase picker from scratch typically requires tens of thousands of labeled examples. Transfer learning with fine-tuning can achieve comparable performance with hundreds. For regional seismic networks where labeled catalogs are incomplete or inconsistently annotated, this is not merely convenient — it is often the only viable path to deployment.
